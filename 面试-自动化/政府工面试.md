## **1️⃣ How do you develop a Test Strategy and Test Plan in a waterfall project?**

**Answer:**

> In a waterfall project, the Test Strategy and Test Plan are developed after the requirements and design are finalized and signed off.  
> I start by reviewing the BRD, SRS, and design documents to understand the full scope.  
> The Test Strategy defines the overall approach — the test levels, types, environments, and risk-based testing focus.  
> The Test Plan details the schedule, entry and exit criteria, resources, and deliverables.  
> Both documents are peer-reviewed and approved by the Test Manager and Project Manager before execution.  
> This ensures testing is structured, aligned with organizational standards, and fully traceable.

---

## **2️⃣ What documents do you review before preparing test cases?**

**Answer:**

> Before writing test cases, I always review key baseline documents such as the BRD, SRS, HLD, and Interface Control Documents.  
> These help me understand both functional and non-functional requirements.  
> I also refer to the RTM to confirm all requirements are covered, and check for any approved Change Requests.  
> Reviewing these ensures that every test case directly maps to a signed-off requirement and that nothing is missed.

---

## **3️⃣ How do you ensure requirements are testable?**

**Answer:**

> In a waterfall project, once requirements are baselined, I participate in the requirement review sessions with BAs and developers.  
> I check each requirement for clarity, completeness, and testability — for example, whether it has clear input, output, and measurable acceptance criteria.  
> If any requirement is ambiguous, I raise a clarification log or requirement defect.  
> This proactive review helps avoid gaps later when test cases are developed.

---

## **4️⃣ How do you handle defects found during system testing?**

**Answer:**

> All defects are logged in our defect management tool — usually Jira or HP ALM — with detailed reproduction steps, screenshots, and severity.  
> We follow a structured defect life cycle: New → Assigned → Fixed → Retested → Closed.  
> A daily defect triage meeting is held with Dev, BA, and Test Lead to prioritize and resolve issues.  
> Severity and priority are assigned based on business impact, following the organization’s defect severity matrix.  
> This ensures transparency and timely resolution in line with waterfall governance.

---

## **5️⃣ What would you do if test cases or plan are not sufficient during testing?**

**Answer:**

> If I discover gaps in the test plan or missing test cases, I first perform an impact analysis to identify affected requirements using the RTM.  
> I immediately raise the issue to the Test Manager and Project Manager.  
> Following waterfall principles, I submit a formal change request to update the Test Plan and add new test cases.  
> After sign-off, I execute the additional tests and update the RTM and Test Summary Report.  
> This ensures compliance with the controlled change process and maintains audit traceability.

---

## **6️⃣ How do you ensure your test artifacts align with the organization’s QA framework?**

**Answer:**

> I always start by reviewing the organization’s QA policies, templates, and testing standards.  
> All my key artifacts — Test Strategy, Test Plan, RTM, and Test Reports — are created using approved templates and stored in controlled repositories like SharePoint.  
> Each artifact goes through internal peer review and formal sign-off by the QA Manager.  
> I also participate in QA audits to ensure consistency and compliance.  
> This alignment guarantees that our documentation meets the organization’s governance and audit requirements.

---

## **7️⃣ How do you report testing progress to stakeholders?**

**Answer:**

> I provide structured progress reports during each testing phase — usually through Daily Status Updates and Weekly Test Summary Reports.  
> The reports include test execution progress, defect metrics, risks, and upcoming activities.  
> In waterfall projects, these reports feed into formal phase-gate reviews, such as the System Test Exit meeting.  
> This transparent communication keeps the PM, BA, and business stakeholders informed, supporting timely decisions on release readiness.

---

## **8️⃣ How do you handle late requirement changes in a waterfall project?**

**Answer:**

> In waterfall, requirements are normally frozen after sign-off, so any change must go through formal change control.  
> If a new or modified requirement arises, I first assess its impact on the test scope, test data, and schedule.  
> Then I raise a Change Request with justification and updated estimates.  
> Once approved, I update the RTM, Test Plan, and affected test cases.  
> This ensures all changes are properly documented, approved, and traceable through the governance framework.
